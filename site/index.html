World Cup 2026 — Chemistry

Can on-pitch chemistry signals predict whether a player is sharper for his club than his country? An honest investigation grounded in Bransen & Van Haaren’s 2020 JOI/JDI framework, extended with network-position and familiarity signals across six international tournaments and eight club seasons.

The motivation

Chemistry is intuitive but unmeasured

When a club signs a new player, scouts evaluate individual quality — goals, ratings, physical profile. They rarely measure how a signing will combine with the players already on the squad. Yet the same forward who excels at one club can struggle at another, not because of fitness or form, but because the system and teammates do not suit his movement patterns.

Bransen & Van Haaren posed the question formally: can we quantify how well any two players combine, and predict it for pairs who have never yet played together?

What they built

  • Two metrics: JOI (offensive) and JDI (defensive)
  • Regression models to predict chemistry for unseen pairs
  • A mixed-integer Team Builder to assemble optimal squads
Core concept

What counts as an interaction?

Chemistry is grounded in interactions — the smallest unit of collaboration between two players. An interaction between players p and q in a match is simply two consecutive on-ball actions, one by each player, within the same possession:

Interaction(p, q) = (action by p, followed immediately by action by q)

Concrete examples: p plays a through-ball and q receives it; p wins a header and q collects the second ball; p cuts inside and q finishes. Both orderings count — p→q and q→p.

The interaction score is the sum of the VAEP (or xT, or xG-chain) values of those two actions. A sharp pass into the box that creates a clear chance carries a high combined score. A misplaced ball that gifts possession carries a negative one.

Metric: JOI

Joint Offensive Impact (JOI90)

JOI sums the value of every interaction a pair produces across a match — in both directions — then normalises to 90 minutes of shared pitch time so that pairs with different amounts of shared time can be compared fairly:

JOI_match(p, q) = sum of VAEP over all p→q interactions + sum of VAEP over all q→p interactions
JOI90(p, q) = sum of JOI_match across all matches × 90 / total shared minutes

A positive JOI90 means the pair's combined on-ball actions consistently improve their team's probability of scoring. Higher is better; the scale depends on the underlying action-value model.

Minimum minutes filter

This site requires at least 180 shared minutes before a pair appears in the leaderboard. The original paper uses 700 minutes for its season-level analysis; we relax this for international tournaments because squad rotation limits shared time.

Metric: JDI (not yet implemented)

Joint Defensive Impact — deferred

The paper's second metric, JDI, measures how much a defending pair suppresses their opponents' offensive output relative to expectation. The logic: if an attacker consistently underperforms his season-average impact while facing a specific pair, that pair deserves defensive credit.

Why it is harder

Event data does not record what a defender prevented — only what attackers did. JDI therefore relies on an expected offensive impact baseline per opponent, built by tracking their per-90 VAEP across all prior matches in the window.

Responsibility is allocated by proximity: each pair's share of credit against an attacker is proportional to how close their default positions are to the attacker's position on a 5×5 grid.

Why we deferred it

International windows are short. With only 4 to 7 matches per tournament, the expected-impact baseline is noisy for players with limited caps. Incorporating multiple tournaments into a rolling baseline requires careful handling of tournament-level opponents, which is on the roadmap but not yet done.

StatsBomb events → SPADL

SPADL (Soccer Player Action Description Language) is a unified representation of on-ball actions. The socceraction library converts StatsBomb's raw event feed into a standardised sequence of passes, crosses, dribbles, take-ons, and shots — each tagged with player, team, location, and result.

Action value

Each SPADL action is scored by one of three models. The score expresses how much that action shifted the team's probability of scoring (or, for VAEP, the probability of a goal in the next few actions). See the Metric comparison tab for detail on each model.

Pair interactions → JOI90

For every match, the SPADL sequence is scanned to find consecutive-action pairs. Each action's value is summed into the JOI for the pair it belongs to. After all matches in the rolling window, each pair's total JOI is scaled to 90 shared minutes.

Scope of this project

Six men's international tournaments. Original four-tournament window: FIFA World Cup 2022, UEFA Euro 2024, UEFA Euro 2020, Copa América 2024. Added to expand coverage for Africa and WC 2018 squads: FIFA World Cup 2018, AFCON 2023. 314 matches. ~653K SPADL actions.

Adaptation

How this project diverges from the original paper

Data source

Bransen & Van Haaren used Wyscout club match data across 361 seasons in 106 competitions. This project uses StatsBomb Open Data — freely available event feeds for international tournaments only.

Scope: international not club

Club chemistry accumulates across a full season (700+ shared minutes). International chemistry is thinner — top pairs meet for 300–600 minutes across 4–7 matches per tournament. We compensate with a rolling multi-tournament window rather than a single season.

Rosters

Where May 2026 call-ups or announced final squads are available, those are used. Otherwise, rosters are auto-derived from each nation's highest-capped players in the six-tournament corpus.

Three metric views

The original paper uses VAEP exclusively. This site adds two alternatives: xT (a non-parametric zone-transition model) and xG-Chain (possession-credit). This lets you see whether a pair's chemistry is robust across approaches or appears only under a single model's assumptions.

No prediction model

The paper's predictive layer — CatBoost regression to estimate chemistry for unseen pairs — requires proprietary SciSports features (22 Player Roles, Physical Performance Indicators). Those are not available in the open data used here, so the prediction step is omitted. Rankings are observational only.

No Team Builder

Without predictions for unseen pairs, the mixed-integer Team Builder optimisation cannot be run in its original form. The nation-level pitch charts serve as a partial substitute, showing the observed chemistry network for each squad.

Reading the paper carefully

What JOI/JDI actually measures — and what they don't

The paper is precise about its operating assumption: "two players who have high mutual chemistry perform better than two players who have low mutual chemistry, all other things being equal." That phrase — all other things being equal — is the entire crux of the chemistry problem, and the metrics never operationalise it.

JOI is joint productivity, not synergy

JOI90 sums VAEP across a pair's consecutive actions, normalised per 90 shared minutes. There is no baseline subtraction for individual quality. Two elite forwards who share attacking minutes will post a high JOI whether or not they have any chemistry above what their individual abilities predict. Salah–Firmino top the 2017/18 Champions League rankings — but how much of that 0.7077 is unique synergy vs. two great forwards in a great attacking system?

JDI's prediction model barely beats the baseline

The paper trains a CatBoost regressor to predict JDI90 for unseen pairs. Test-set RMSE: 0.88906 for the model vs. 0.89075 for a naïve mean baseline — a 0.2% improvement. Defensive chemistry is, by their own numbers, essentially unpredictable from features. JOI prediction is meaningfully better (18%) but still leaves most variance unexplained.

Cultural features hardly matter

The paper notes: same nationality, same mother tongue, same region "exhibit only limited predictive power in both models." So much for the "shared language gels the dressing room" intuition — at least at the on-ball action level.

Implication for this site

The Charts tab ranks pairs by their joint VAEP. Read that as: "which pairs combined to produce on-ball value most efficiently per shared minute." It is informative — it surfaces the players who actually create together. It is not a measurement of synergy net of individual quality. That distinction matters when comparing a player's pairs at club vs. country.

Related research

Frameworks that try to isolate synergy

Other researchers — mostly in basketball, where lineup data is richer — have built frameworks that explicitly separate individual quality from teammate effects. These point at what a more honest "chemistry" measurement would look like.

Maymin, Maymin & Shen (2013) — NBA SPM

"NBA Chemistry: Positive and Negative Synergies in Basketball." Fits nested probit regressions over 4,718 games and 987,343 plays. Every player gets coefficients across three skill categories (scoring, rebounding, ball-handling) jointly with a replacement-level baseline. Synergy of a lineup = simulated 5-player effectiveness minus the sum of individual point-differentials. The first explicit "above sum-of-parts" decomposition in pro sports. PDF

Kuehn (2016) — complementary skill sets

MIT Sloan. A probabilistic possession model where event probabilities depend separately on individual skills and teammate complementarities. Conclusion: NBA teams pay players for individual output and systematically under-value complementarity. MIT Sloan abstract

Beal, Changder, Norman & Ramchurn (2020) — teamwork value

AAAI-20. Constructs a directed weighted graph of inter-player passes, then values agents and pairs via three network metrics — centrality, distance-from-event, and walk frequency — learned against match-outcome labels. Their "interactional alignment" concept rewards pairs whose own value is high and whose connections to the rest of the team are strong. Validated on 64 games from the 2018 World Cup; pair-value model is up to 46% more accurate at predicting team-level passes than singleton-only baselines. AAAI proceedings

American Soccer Analysis (Jan 2026) — career familiarity

Brian Boer at ASA proposes a much simpler cohesion proxy: the running total of passes between players over their careers. Regressed against xG / xGA at team level alongside player-quality and minutes-played controls. Acknowledges possession-heavy bias and survivorship bias; modest explanatory power but cheap to compute. Chemistry 101

Where this leaves us

Back to the drawing board

The original anchor for this project was the Podolski paradox — a player who was a "monster for Germany, shit show for his clubs" — and the question of whether the 2014 Germany side over-performed because so many of them already played together at Bayern. JOI was an obvious tool to reach for. The careful reading above is the reason it's the wrong one.

What we can't claim from JOI alone

  • That a player's chemistry "exists at one team but not the other." Their JOI changes between contexts because teammates' quality changes, opposition strength changes, tactical role changes, score state changes — and JOI has no controls for any of those.
  • That a per-90 VAEP drop from club to country reflects lost chemistry. It could equally reflect lost service, harder opposition, or a deeper role.
  • That the paper's own metric supports "chemistry transfers from club to international" — the cultural-feature null result in the original work actively undercuts that hypothesis.

What might actually work

  • Network position, not network value. Compare each player's centrality in their club passing graph vs. their country graph. A player who sits at the hub at one level and on the periphery at the other tells the chemistry story visually, without pretending to isolate synergy.
  • Interactional alignment (Beal). Score pairs by how their connections embed in the rest of the team. This penalises pairs that "play in isolation" — closer to what fans mean by "the team gels around them."
  • Familiarity as a control, not the answer. Career pass counts between teammates (per ASA) are cheap and intuitive. Useful as a covariate when asking "does extra minutes-together actually predict joint output above what individual quality predicts?"
  • Accept that synergy may not be cleanly isolable from event data alone. Tracking data and lineup-rotation density are what make the basketball frameworks work. Football has neither in equal measure.

The Charts and Research tabs on this site still show what they show — observed joint VAEP, observed per-context production. Read them honestly: as descriptions of who combined to produce value, not as evidence that chemistry caused the difference. The next iteration of this project will pivot toward network-position framing, which is more defensible with the data we actually have.

Overview

Three ways to value an action

Each metric on this site is a different answer to the same question: how much did this pass, dribble, or shot improve the team's attacking position? They agree on direction — all three reward progressive, dangerous play — but they disagree on scale, which actions to credit most, and how to handle possession that does not end in a shot.

xT — Expected Threat

Definition

Divides the pitch into a grid of zones and estimates the probability that a possession starting in each zone will produce a goal within the next few actions. An action's xT value is the probability gain from moving the ball from one zone to a better one.

What it rewards highest

Ball progression into the final third, especially crosses and through-balls that shift the ball from low-threat to high-threat zones.

Stability / data demand

Most stable of the three. The zone-transition matrix requires only shot outcomes and carry endpoints — no training labels per action needed.

Known failure mode

Over-credits patient backline buildup. A central defender who recycles possession from his own 18-yard box gains positive xT on every successful pass, even if the team never threatened the opponent's goal. CB–CB and CB–GK pairs therefore rank disproportionately high.

Citation

Karun Singh (2019). karun.in/blog/expected-threat

VAEP — Valuing Actions by Estimating Probabilities

Definition

A trained binary classifier predicts, after each action, the probability that the team scores and concedes in the next few seconds. VAEP is the change in those probabilities caused by the action. Negative values are possible — a misplaced pass that gifts possession has a negative score.

What it rewards highest

Actions that directly raise the probability of an imminent goal: shots on target, key passes, and take-ons that beat the last defender.

Stability / data demand

Requires training labels from the event sequence. More data-hungry than xT; the model trained here on ~653K actions is at the smaller end of what VAEP is designed for.

Known failure mode

Absolute scale is small: with only ~4,300 goal events in the training set the probability deltas are compressed. Rankings across pairs are reliable; the raw JOI90 values are not directly comparable to a club-data VAEP.

Citation

Decroos, Bransen, Van Haaren & Davis (2019). Actions Speak Louder than Goals. KDD '19.

xG-Chain

Definition

Credits every player who touched the ball in a possession chain with the expected goals (xG) value of the shot that ended the chain. If a possession ends without a shot, no credit is distributed.

What it rewards highest

Active participation in possessions that produce shots. A player who completes three passes in a move ending with a goal-mouth opportunity earns the full xG of that opportunity.

Stability / data demand

Lightest model of the three — requires only shot xG values and possession chain membership. The xG model here is a logistic regression on shot location and body part (~5K shots), so it is directionally correct but conservative on absolute values.

Known failure mode

Attributes possession credit very broadly. A midfielder who makes a routine pass early in a long build-up earns the same credit as the player who delivered the final ball. Pairs benefit from being on the pitch during productive teams regardless of their own direct contribution.

Citation

Widely used in the public analytics community; no single canonical paper. Popularised via StatsBomb and FBref.

Convergence

Where the metrics agree

At the very top of the leaderboard the three metrics converge. Pairs that are elite under all three models tend to be creative attackers who combine frequently in the final third, produce shots, and accumulate shared minutes through consistent tournament selection.

Agreement: Neymar + Vinícius Júnior (BRA)

xT rank: #1  |  VAEP rank: #3

Two attackers who combine on the left channel across multiple tournaments. High progressive carry value (xT), high probability-of-goal shifts on their interactions (VAEP), and frequent involvement in shot-producing possessions (xG-Chain). All three models agree this is a genuine elite partnership.

Agreement: Neymar + Raphinha (BRA)

xT rank: #20  |  VAEP rank: #2

Another Brazilian attacking pair that ranks in the top tier across all three views. The xT rank is somewhat lower because Raphinha's wide starting position means some of their interactions begin in mid-table threat zones; VAEP and xG-Chain are better at crediting the quality of the outcome rather than the zone entry point.

When two or three metrics all place a pair in the top 20, that is a reliable signal. A pair appearing in the top 10 under only one model warrants closer inspection of what that model rewards.

Divergence

Where the metrics disagree — a real example

The clearest illustration of xT's backline-buildup problem comes from the Uruguay centre-back pairing.

Diego Godín + Sergio Rochet (URU)  —  CB + GK

xT rank: #5  |  VAEP rank: #42

Godín (CB) and Rochet (GK) share a large number of short-range exchanges in defensive buildup — goal kicks, short passes back to goalkeeper, sideways movement out of defensive pressure. Every successful pass between them increments their xT score because the ball moves to a zone with non-zero goal probability. xT does not distinguish between recycled possession at the back and a genuine threat-creating move.

VAEP evaluates the same exchanges and sees almost no change in the probability of scoring in the next few seconds. A goalkeeper returning the ball to a centre-back 35 yards from goal does not meaningfully raise the team's scoring probability, so VAEP leaves those interactions with near-zero credit — ranking this pair at #42 instead of #5.

Pattern: CB-CB and CB-GK pairs

Several other defensive pairs show the same xT inflation: Australia's Harry Souttar + Kye Rowles (xT #10, VAEP #34) and the Netherlands' Virgil van Dijk + Jurriën Timber (xT #26, VAEP #39). If you are looking for genuine attacking chemistry rather than ball-retention proficiency, VAEP or xG-Chain is a more reliable guide for defensive pairs.

When to trust which

  • Attacking pairs — all three metrics broadly agree. Use xT for quick scans; check VAEP for a secondary view.
  • Defensive pairs — VAEP and xG-Chain are more reliable; ignore xT rankings above #20 for CB/GK combinations.
  • Wide midfielder + fullback combos — xT and xG-Chain tend to agree; VAEP adds nuance on whether their combinations end in shots.

Findings

The cross-context question

Fans have a gut feeling that some players are “club players” — sharper for their employer than their country — while others are the reverse. The cleanest version of the question is: when a player produces less (or more) VAEP per 90 in the national-team shirt than at his club, can on-pitch chemistry signals — who he passes with, how central he is to the pass network, how interconnected his closest partners are — predict the direction and size of that gap? We tested this on every player in StatsBomb open data with both a club and an international footprint: 109 dual-context players, 2,164 player-context rows, 24,213 career-familiarity pairs.

The major correction

Most of the “club hero” gap was an opposition artifact

Domestic-league fixtures include many weak opponents (Eibar, Las Palmas, Clermont, Troyes) that inflate the club VAEP numerator; international tournament fixtures are uniformly elite. If the cross-context gap is largely an opposition-strength artifact, restricting the club side to elite-opponent matches only should shrink it. We don’t have Champions League event data in the open StatsBomb corpus, so we built a CL-proxy by subsetting:

  • Barcelona La Liga 17/18–20/21: matches vs Real Madrid and Atlético Madrid only (15 matches across four seasons).
  • PSG Ligue 1 21/22–22/23: matches vs Marseille, Monaco, and Lyon (9 matches across two seasons).

Per-90 VAEP is then recomputed on the elite-opp subset only and compared to each player’s international tournament rate.

The result

Across the 10 dual-context Barça/PSG players, the mean club−country gap drops from +0.204 VAEP/90 on the full domestic sample to +0.014 on the elite-opp subset — a ~93% reduction. Six of the ten players flip from positive to zero-or-negative gaps. The “club hero, national let-down” framing largely doesn’t survive once you compare like opposition to like opposition.

Two takeaways

  • Messi is the genuine exception. His gap shrinks from +0.599 to +0.198 but stays positive — he is sharper at Barcelona than for Argentina even against Real Madrid + Atlético.
  • Mbappé’s story reverses. Against OM / Monaco / Lyon at PSG he produces 0.326 VAEP/90 — below his France tournament rate of 0.447. The “PSG hero, France passenger” reading was wrong; he is at least as good at France as against elite Ligue 1 sides.

When the metric misreads the player

Lionel Messi — the case per-90 VAEP gets wrong

Messi has the largest cross-context per-90 VAEP gap in the corpus: +0.85 at Barcelona / Inter Miami vs. +0.26 at Argentina across 17,000 club minutes and 1,464 international minutes. Every chemistry signal sags with him — ΔJOI top-5 −0.47, Δcentrality −0.07, Δembeddedness −1.86. If you read the metric at face value, he is the biggest “club hero, national let-down” in the dataset.

He is not. Messi at the 2022 World Cup scored 7 goals, registered 3 assists, won the Golden Ball, and lifted the trophy. Across his three tournaments in the data (WC 18, WC 22, Copa 24) he produced more decisive moments than any other player in the corpus. The framing “under-performs at country” is wrong for him on every meaningful definition of the word.

What the metric is actually catching is a denominator effect: at Barcelona he touched the ball 90+ times per match in a possession-dominant system, so his per-90 numerator (total VAEP) is spread over a huge action stream and the rate is high. At Argentina the team plays more directly, possession sequences are shorter, and his per-match action count is a fraction of his club rate. The decisive actions he does take — the goals, the through-balls, the shootout strikes — are world-class. The per-90 rate just can’t see them.

This is the honest limit of every per-90 action-value framework, including the paper’s. VAEP averages across all on-ball actions. Tournament football is about the rare actions that decide matches. Messi is the cleanest case where those two definitions of “productive” come apart.

One genuine finding survives the opposition-strength control above: even when we restrict the club side to matches vs. Real Madrid + Atlético Madrid (24 matches, ~2,100 minutes), Messi’s per-90 VAEP is 0.460 — still meaningfully above his Argentina tournament rate of 0.262. Whatever the per-90 misses about his decisive moments, the raw on-ball production rate is higher at Barcelona than at Argentina — even against the best opposition La Liga offered. He is the one player in the corpus where the club-vs-country gap is real, not an artifact.

The case that breaks the story

Patrik Schick — chemistry signals pointing the wrong way

Schick is the second-largest over-performer at country in the corpus: per-90 VAEP of 0.768 for the Czech Republic vs. 0.164 at Bayer Leverkusen 23/24 — a delta of +0.60 in 405 international minutes and 1,064 club minutes. If chemistry were the explanation, you’d expect his JOI top-5 sum and embeddedness to rise alongside.

They don’t. His JOI top-5 sum drops by 0.70 internationally, and his embeddedness drops by 3.69 — one of the largest negative embeddedness deltas in the dataset. Yet his per-90 VAEP nearly quintuples. Centrality is the only signal that nudges up (+0.15).

This is what makes the chemistry-as-explanation story fragile. A model that says “more JOI with your strongest partners ⇒ better individual production” predicts Schick should drop off for the Czechs, not surge.

Methodology

How the 2026 squads are ranked

Each WC 2026 squad gets a single composite z-score built from four signals on its current 26-man roster:

  • Mean club centrality — the average eigenvector centrality of each squad member in his current club’s success-weighted pass graph. Higher means the squad is built from hubs, not peripherals.
  • Intra-squad familiarity — the normalised sum of career shared minutes between every pair of squadmates, drawn from career_familiarity.parquet (24,213 pairs). Captures how much pitch time these 26 players have logged together over their careers.
  • Shared-club density — the share of squadmate pairs that have at least one club in common in their career history. A proxy for club-system overlap.
  • Observed pair JOI90 — the mean per-90 Joint Offensive Impact among squad pairs that have actually played together in our six-tournament corpus.

Each signal is z-scored across the 48 squads, then the four z-scores are averaged into the composite. Squads where fewer than 60% of called-up players match our event-data corpus are flagged with a low-coverage warning — their numbers should be read directionally.

The honest caveat

Why England’s #1 is partly a coverage artefact

StatsBomb’s open club releases tilt heavily toward La Liga (Barcelona-era), Ligue 1 (PSG-era), the Bundesliga (Leverkusen 23/24), and MLS (Inter Miami). Premier League players are not directly covered at the club level — but they are over-represented in the international tournaments we score (England has six tournaments worth of data; Algeria has none). That asymmetry inflates England’s intra-squad familiarity score: the Three Lions have played together for so many international windows that their career-pair minutes dwarf squads we rarely see.

The honest claim is: among the squads we can measure, England has the strongest chemistry signals. It is not “England is the chemistry favourite for WC 2026”. Squads with deep club rosters that we can’t observe — Italy was excluded entirely; Austria, Norway, and Iran sit in the −0.77 floor because we’ve never drafted them — could plausibly rank above any of the top five if the data were symmetric.

The cleanest read of this ranking is the Germany card. Three Leverkusen 23/24 teammates — Xhaka, Tah, Wirtz — entering Nagelsmann’s setup with 2,500+ minutes of club-side chemistry behind them is the kind of finding that doesn’t depend on the data-coverage asymmetry. It would be there whatever the rest of the sample looked like.
